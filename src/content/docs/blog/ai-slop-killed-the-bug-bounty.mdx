A real open-source database project just shut down its $1,000-per-critical-bug
bounty program. The program had run for almost a year. It worked great. Then
this week it ended — not because the budget ran out, but because LLM-generated
"vulnerabilities" flooded the maintainer's inbox faster than humans could
triage them.

The pattern is bigger than one project. It's the second-order effect of cheap
inference meeting open incentive systems, and it's coming for every queue,
every form, every public bounty board on the internet.

## The contract that worked

A bug bounty is a beautifully simple contract. The project says: _if you find
a bug that meets this severity bar, we'll pay you $X._ That contract trades
one scarce resource (engineer time) for another (skilled adversarial
attention). Both sides win. The project gets a security audit they couldn't
otherwise afford. The hunter gets paid for genuinely useful work.

That contract has a hidden assumption: the cost of *submitting* a report is
roughly the cost of *producing* one. Producing a real exploit is hard.
Writing it up takes effort. So submission volume self-throttles to roughly
the rate of real findings.

Cheap LLM inference broke that assumption.

## What changed

It costs about $0.001 to ask a frontier model to generate a "vulnerability
report" against a codebase. The model will happily oblige. It will produce
text that looks like a security finding — references to memory safety, a
plausible CVE format, a call-to-action paragraph. The report will often be
completely fictional. The "exploit" will not reproduce. The "vulnerable
code path" will not exist. But it will look real enough that a human has to
spend ten minutes reading it before reaching that conclusion.

For a bounty program, that asymmetry is fatal. Submission is now ~$0 and
triage is now ~$10 of maintainer attention. The maintainer is paying with
the most expensive resource a software project has, and the slop submitter
is paying with the cheapest.

It only takes a small number of automated submitters to overwhelm a small
team. And once the submission rate exceeds the triage rate, the queue grows
forever.

## "We automated finding bugs. Then submitting them. Then rejecting them."

You see this same shape everywhere now:

- **PR spam on popular OSS repos.** Bots open hundreds of "drive-by" pull
  requests against trending projects. Most are LLM-generated typo fixes or
  hallucinated refactors. Maintainers either auto-close everything (losing
  legitimate contributions) or burn out triaging.
- **Issue spam in support trackers.** Auto-filed "I think there might be a
  bug" reports with no reproduction, often produced by a chatbot middle
  layer that promised the user it would "report it to the developers."
- **Comment spam dressed as substantive critique.** The flood now reads
  better. It's harder to filter. It's still empty.
- **Code review spam.** A PR shows up with thirty AI-generated review
  comments, most of which are either wrong, irrelevant, or tautologies
  ("consider whether this function should handle null").

We automated finding bugs. Then we automated submitting bugs. Now we're
automating *rejecting* bugs. Nobody automated *fixing* them.

## The real cost isn't tokens

The interesting thing isn't the inference bill. It's that the cost of the
attack is paid by the attacker, but the cost of the defense is paid by the
defender — and the defender's resource is intrinsically more expensive.

You cannot LLM your way out of an LLM-generated triage queue, because the
triage decision is the *exact thing* that requires the human judgment the
attacker is trying to commodify. If you put an AI triage layer in front of
the queue, you've just created a new game: produce reports that are
plausible enough to pass the AI filter. That game is also cheap to play.

Maintainer attention is the bottleneck. It always was. Every layer of
automation that you bolt onto a maintainer's workflow eventually has to
return a decision to them: *should I read this, fix this, merge this,
reject this.* That decision is what AI slop attacks. The decision queue is
the soft underbelly of every open community.

## What might actually work

A few patterns are starting to show up in maintainer conversations. None
are silver bullets:

1. **Refundable deposit to submit.** A small refundable deposit, returned
   if the report is valid, forfeited if it's slop. This re-prices the
   attacker's side without affecting genuine hunters. The downside: it
   filters out hobbyist newcomers who don't have a credit card on file.

2. **Reputation-gated submissions.** First-time submitters can only file
   reports that meet a much higher bar (full POC, video reproduction,
   maintainer-vouched introduction). Established hunters bypass the bar.
   This rebuilds the apprenticeship model, but raises the cost of
   discovering new contributors.

3. **Honeypot programs.** Projects publish fake bounty pages designed to
   attract automated scanners. The submissions train classifiers on what
   slop looks like in your specific ecosystem. Several maintainers have
   started doing this informally; one popular pattern is a public
   "Bounty Hunters" repo with a leaderboard of automated submitters,
   which both shames the spammer and crowdsources detection.

4. **Move bounties off public surfaces entirely.** Run them through
   verified-identity programs (HackerOne, Bugcrowd) where the platform
   handles attribution and gating. The trade-off: small projects can't
   afford the platform fees, and you lose the discovery effect of an
   open bounty.

5. **"Proof of code."** Require a working PR against a related part of
   the codebase before you can submit a bounty report. This converts
   cheap-to-fake LLM output into harder-to-fake actual engineering. It
   doesn't solve the problem, but it shifts the asymmetry.

Whatever wins, it will look different from the open-bounty contract that
worked for the last twenty years.

## The pattern is bigger than bounties

The same dynamic is going to play out — is already playing out — anywhere
you have an open queue with humans on the other side:

- Customer support tickets
- Open peer review on academic papers
- Comment sections on news articles
- Public consultation periods on regulation
- Open hiring channels (LinkedIn DMs, careers@ inboxes)
- Conference paper submissions
- Government FOIA requests

Anywhere the cost of producing input is collapsing while the cost of
processing that input stays human-bounded, the queue eventually breaks.

The economic concept is well-understood — it's the tragedy of the commons,
just on the *attention* commons instead of the grazing commons. What's new
is the rate. The cost of producing plausible-looking text used to fall by
order of magnitude per decade. It now falls by order of magnitude per
year. Most institutions designed around the old cost curve don't have
time to redesign before the next cost-of-production drop hits them.

## What I take from this

Two things.

First, the next decade of platform design is going to be about building
*friction back in* on purpose. Identity, deposits, reputation, proof of
work. We spent twenty years removing friction from public-facing systems
because friction was the enemy of growth. Now friction is the only thing
that protects the people on the receiving end of those systems. The
companies that figure out how to add friction without losing the magic
will eat the rest.

Second, "AI agents" is becoming a load-bearing word that people use to
mean two completely different things:

- **Slop pipelines.** Cheap inference pointed at any open queue, with no
  judgment loop, generating volume that humans then have to filter.
- **Useful agents.** A tool a human uses to do their actual work better
  — drive a browser, read their codebase, edit a file, run a command.

Conflating these two categories is bad for everyone in the second one.
The first category is what most people actually mean when they say "AI
slop." The second category is what most people *building* agentic tools
actually want to ship.

If you're shipping in the second category, the most important thing you
can do right now is make it visible that there's a human at the other end
of every action your tool takes. The slop pipelines are not human-at-the-
other-end. That distinction is going to become a brand promise.
